I want to build a web-based word game called WordDrop, and I want you to build it end to end — every file, the game logic, the backend, the deployment setup — production-ready, not a prototype or a sketch.
Here's the experience I'm going for. Someone opens the game on their phone. No loading wall, no forced sign-up. They land on a home screen, pick a category and a difficulty, and they're playing within two taps. The feeling I care about most is frictionless and fast. It should work beautifully in a mobile browser, automatically support light and dark mode based on the device setting, and feel like a real app even though it's just a shared link.
The heart of the game is the grid. A 6x6 board of letters with real words hidden in every direction — horizontal, vertical, diagonal, forwards and backwards. The player drags a finger through connected adjacent letters to build a word, and the letters light up as they connect. When they lift their finger, a valid word of at least three letters scores and flashes green; an invalid one shakes and clears. They keep finding words endlessly — there's no cap on how many they can find. The only thing that ends a round is the timer hitting zero. A word that's already been found can't be scored a second time.
The single most important part of this whole build is the grid generation, so I want you to treat it as the core engineering problem, not an afterthought. A grid of truly random letters would have no findable words and would kill the game instantly. So the generator has to work backwards: it must first select real words from the chosen category — up to twelve words, every one of which must physically fit inside the 6x6 grid — and place them into the board in varied directions. Only after the real words are placed does it fill the remaining empty cells, and that filler must follow natural English letter frequency (far more E, A, R, S, T than Q, Z, X) so the board looks organic rather than obviously seeded. Done right, the filler will sometimes form accidental bonus words, which is part of the fun. Every finished grid should be sanity-checked so no inappropriate words end up spelled out. And for validating what a player traces, bundle an open-source English dictionary (such as the dwyl words_alpha list) directly into the app so word checks are instant and never need a server round-trip. The grid must always make sense — that's the standard this lives or dies by.
Scoring runs by word length: three letters is 10 points, four is 20, five is 40, and six or more is 60. Keep that scoring logic in its own module so it's easy to tune later.
The categories at launch are Animals, Countries, Names, General, Food & Drinks, Sports, Cities, and Random. Food & Drinks should be rich and include both Nigerian and international foods that people actually know. Sports should include sporting terms and equipment, not just sport names. Random mixes words from across all categories — anything goes. I'll add more categories later, so structure the word lists as separate, easily extendable data files. Difficulty changes both the timer and the letter difficulty: Easy gives a generous timer and friendly, common letters; Normal is balanced; Hard gives a short timer and trickier filler that nearly forms words to bait wrong guesses.
There are two kinds of users, and the distinction matters. Registered players sign up with an email and a display name — the email feeds my marketing list, the display name is what appears on leaderboards, and display names must be unique. If a player doesn't want to invent one, let them tap to auto-generate a unique name and accept it instantly. Guests are different: a guest plays as a pure ghost. Nothing they do is saved, they don't appear on any leaderboard, and they can't share. A guest simply picks a category and difficulty and plays for fun. That's the whole guest experience — no storage, no ranking, no sharing.
The growth loop is grid sharing, and only registered players can share. Every grid a player generates carries a unique session ID embedded in a shareable link. When a player finishes a round, they see their own score first, then a share button — and sharing is also available during play. Anyone who opens that link loads the exact same grid: same letters, same category, same difficulty, and plays to beat the original sharer. That grid's leaderboard shows everyone who has attempted it. Sessions expire three hours after creation — we can't keep them open forever. If someone opens a link after it's expired, they shouldn't hit a dead end; instead they drop into their own fresh onboarding where they can play as a guest or register, just starting clean.
For the backend, use Supabase with two tables. One for sessions, storing the grid layout, category, difficulty, session ID, and creation timestamp. One for scores, storing session ID, display name, score, number of words found, and time taken. The shareable link carries the session ID, which loads the stored grid and pulls that session's leaderboard.
Build the game board, the swipe-to-connect detection, and the animations in Phaser.js. Use plain JavaScript for the home screen, onboarding, and routing between screens. Use Supabase for all storage. Structure everything to deploy cleanly on Vercel with no heavy framework and no unnecessary build complexity. I want clean, well-organized, commented code that I can actually read and maintain, with the grid generator, the word lists, and the scoring logic each living in their own modules so they're simple to tune and extend.
